Variance of the estimator under Heteroskedasticity

Consider the simple linear regression model:

$$ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + u_i, \quad i = 1, \dots, n $$

where the error terms $u_i$ may be heteroskedastic:

$$ \mathrm{Var}(u_i) = \sigma_i^2 \neq \sigma^2 \ \text{(may vary with $i$)}. $$

The heteroskedasticity-robust (Eicker-Huber-White) variance of $\hat\beta_1$ is given by:

$$ \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_1) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2 u_i^2}{\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2\right)^2} $$

or, in practice, using the squared residuals $\hat u_i^2$ from the estimated regression:

$$ \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat\beta_1) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2 \hat u_i^2}{\mathtt{SST}_x^2}, \quad \mathtt{SST}_x = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2. $$
Estimated variance $\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat\beta_1)$ vs the true variance $\mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_1)$

  1. Consistency:
    Under standard assumptions (linear model, independent observations, finite fourth moments of $u_i$),
    $\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat\beta_1) \xrightarrow{p} \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_1)$ as $n \to \infty$.
    That is, it converges in probability to the true variance of the OLS estimator.


  2. Usage: estimated residuals:
    In practice, we replace the unknown $u_i^2$ with the squared OLS residuals $\hat u_i^2$.
    This substitution introduces some small-sample bias, but the estimator remains asymptotically valid.

  3. Usage: testing:
    The robust variance provides a correct basis for t-tests and confidence intervals in large samples incase of heteroskedasticity.

Heteroskedasticity-Robust Variance in Multiple Regression

General multiple regression model:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \dots + \beta_k x_k + u $$

where $u$ may be heteroskedastic: $\mathrm{Var}(u_i) \neq \sigma^2$.

A heteroskedasticity-robust (Eicker-Huber-White) estimator of the variance of $\hat\beta_j$ is given by:

$$ \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat\beta_j) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat r_{ij}^2 \hat u_i^2}{(\mathrm{SSR}_j)^2} $$

where:

  • $\hat u_i$ = OLS residual for observation $i$ from the full regression.
  • $\hat r_{ij}$ = residual from regressing $x_j$ on all other regressors ($x_{-j}$), i.e., the portion of $x_j$ orthogonal to other regressors.
  • $\mathrm{SSR}_j = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat r_{ij}^2$ = sum of squared residuals from that auxiliary regression.

Interpretation:

  1. Orthogonalization:
    $\hat r_{ij}^2$ captures the variation in $x_j$ that is independent of other regressors, ensuring we measure the effect of $x_j$ correctly.

  2. Residual weighting:
    $\hat u_i^2$ weights the contribution of each observation by its squared residual, accounting for heteroskedasticity.

  3. Robustness:
    This formula provides a valid variance estimate even if the error variance differs across observations.

  4. Use:
    The robust variance is used to compute t-tests, confidence intervals, and standard errors that remain valid under heteroskedasticity.

  • Question:
    • Show that in the case of homoskedasticity, the standard formula $\displaystyle\mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_j) = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat r_{ij}^2}$ holds

    Assuming homoskedasticity (the first equliaty), $$ \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat\beta_j) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat r_{ij}^2 \sigma^2}{(\mathrm{SSR}_j)^2} = \sigma^2 \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat r_{ij}^2}{(\mathrm{SSR}_j)^2} $$ Use $$ \mathrm{SSR}_j = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat r_{ij}^2 $$ $$ \widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(\hat\beta_j) \approx \sigma^2 \frac{\mathrm{SSR}_j}{(\mathrm{SSR}_j)^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{\mathrm{SSR}_j} $$ Finally, $$ \mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta_j) = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat r_{ij}^2} $$

    Hypothesis testing: H_0: $\beta_j = 0$ against one-sided or two-sided alternative

    The t-statistic is computed as:

    $$ t = \frac{\hat\beta_j - \beta_j^0}{\mathrm{SE}(\hat\beta_j)} $$

    where:

    • $\hat\beta_j$ = OLS estimate of $\beta_j$
    • $\beta_j^0$ = value under $H_0$ (usually $0$)
    • $\mathrm{SE}(\hat\beta_j)$ = standard error of $\hat\beta_j$

    For heteroskedasticity-robust testing, use the robust standard error from the Eicker-Huber-White formula.

    Decision Rule

    1. Compute the t-statistic using the estimated coefficient and its standard error.
    2. Compare $|t|$ to the critical value from the $t$-distribution with $n - k - 1$ degrees of freedom (or $z$ if using robust SE in large samples).
    3. Reject $H_0$ if:
    $$ |t| > t_{n-k-1, 1-\alpha/2} \quad \text{(two-sided)} $$

    or

    $$ t > t_{n-k-1, 1-\alpha} \quad \text{(one-sided, $\beta_j > 0$)} $$

    p-Value Approach

    Alternatively, compute the p-value:

    $$ p = 2 \, P(T_{n-k-1} > |t|) \quad \text{(two-sided)} $$

    Reject $H_0$ if $p < \alpha$.

    # Function formatting the regression outcome; modified to include precision arguments
def format_regression_output(results, precision_p=3, precision_t=3):
    """
    Print a statsmodels OLS regression in Stata-style:
    - Model equation with coefficients
    - Coefficients with standard errors underneath
    - t-statistics and p-values (precision adjustable)
    - Number of observations, SSR, and R-squared

    Parameters
    ----------
    results : statsmodels regression results object
    precision_p : int, optional
        Number of decimal places for p-values (default: 3)
    precision_t : int, optional
        Number of decimal places for t-statistics (default: 3)
    """

    params = results.params
    ses = results.bse
    t_stats = results.tvalues
    p_values = results.pvalues
    dep_var = results.model.endog_names

    # ----- Build regression equation -----
    eq = f"{dep_var} = "

    # Intercept first (if present)
    if "Intercept" in params.index:
        eq += f"{params['Intercept']:.4f}"
    else:
        eq += "0"

    # Add other variables
    for var in params.index:
        if var == "Intercept":
            continue
        sign = "+" if params[var] >= 0 else "-"
        eq += f" {sign} {abs(params[var]):.4f}*{var}"

    print("Model equation:\n")
    print("  " + eq + "\n")

    # ----- Coefficients + SEs + t-stats + p-values -----
    print("Coefficients (standard errors in parentheses, t-statistics and p-values in brackets):\n")
    for var in params.index:
        print(f"{var:12} {params[var]: .4f}")
        print(
            f"{'':12} ({ses[var]:.4f}) "
            f"[t={t_stats[var]:.{precision_t}f}, p={p_values[var]:.{precision_p}f}]"
        )
    print()

    # ----- Model statistics -----
    n = int(results.nobs)
    ssr = float((results.resid ** 2).sum())
    r2 = float(results.rsquared)

    print(f"Number of observations (n): {n}")
    print(f"Sum of squared residuals (SSR): {ssr:.4f}")
    print(f"R-squared: {r2:.4f}\n")

    • BreuschPagan test

    The baseline regression is:

    $$ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{1i} + ... + \beta_k x_{ki} + u_i, $$

    where OLS assumes:

    $$ \operatorname{E}[u_i | X] = 0, \quad \operatorname{Var}(u_i | X) = \sigma^2. $$

    The BP test checks whether the constant-variance assumption is valid.

    Under heteroskedasticity, the variance may follow:

    $$ \operatorname{Var}(u_i | X) = \sigma_i^2, $$

    We use squared residuals as a proxy for the variance:

    $$ \hat{u}_i^2 \sigma_i^2 $$

    If the error variance depends on regressors, then $\hat{u}_i^2$ should be predictable from those regressors.

    So we run an auxiliary regression:

    $$ \hat{u}_i^2 = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 z_{1i} + ... + \alpha_m z_{mi} + v_i $$

    The null hypothesis of homoskedasticity is:

    $$ H_0: \alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = ... = \alpha_m = 0 $$

    Constructing the test statistic:

    Let R^2 be the coefficient of determination from the auxiliary regression of $\hat{u}_i^2$ on z.

    Define the Lagrange Multiplier (LM) statistic:

    $$ LM = n R^2 \quad\text{where n is the sample size.} $$

    Under the null hypothesis:

    $$ LM \sim \chi^2_m $$

    with m degrees of freedom (number of regressors in the auxiliary model excluding the constant).

    Alternative formulation: the F-statistic

    The same auxiliary regression can also be used to form an F-test:

    $$ F = (R^2 / m) / ((1 - R^2)/(n - m - 1)) $$

    which under $H_0$ follows:

    $$ F \sim F_{m, n-m-1} $$

    This is why statsmodels reports both:

    • the LM test ($\chi^2$)
    • the F test ($F$ distribution)

    Weighted Least Squares

    Uncovering mathematics that is beyond numerical algorithms

    Known variation in the variance

    $$ \operatorname{Var}(u \mid x) = \sigma^2 h(x) $$
    Warning:
    • For simplicity, $h(x)$ is a known function
    • If not? will estimate

    Re-writing: $$ \sigma_i^2 = \operatorname{Var}(u_i \mid x_i) = \sigma^2 h(x_i) = \sigma^2 h_i $$

    Change of variables
    $$ x_{ij}^* = x_{ij} / \sqrt{h_i}, \quad\text{$i$ is the observation number} $$

    Then $$ \operatorname{E}\left[\left(\frac{u_i}{\sqrt{h_i}}\right)^2\right] = \frac{1}{h_i} \cdot \sigma^2 h_i = \sigma^2 $$ and \begin{equation} \frac{y_i}{\sqrt{h_i}} = \frac{\beta_0}{h_i} + \frac{\beta_1 x_{i1}}{h_i} + \frac{\beta_2 x_{i2}}{h_i} + \dots + \frac{\beta_k x_{ik}}{h_i} + \frac{u_i}{\sqrt{h_i}} \label{e:wls} \end{equation} You can consider \eqref{e:wls} as the regression equation

    \begin{equation} y^*_i = \tilde{\beta}_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1}^* + \beta_2 x_{i2}^* + \dots + \beta_k x_{ik}^* + v_i \label{e:ols} \end{equation}
  • Question:
    • what is the relationship between $x_{i1}$ and $x_{i1}^*$, $y_i$ and $y^*_i$?

    $x_{i1}^* = x_{i1} / h_i$, $y_i^* = y_i / \sqrt{h_i}$

    • \eqref{e:ols} satisfies the Gauss-Markov theorem
    • $\beta^*_j$ are the estimators, called generalized least squares (GLS)

    estimators

    Info:
  • equation \eqref{e:ols} satisfies all of the ideal assumptions
  • t and F statistics can all be obtained from regressions using the transformed variables
  • The sum of squared residuals from {e:ols} divided by the degrees of freedom is an unbiased estimator of $s^2$
  • the GLS estimators, because they are the best linear unbiased estimators of the $b_j$, are necessarily more efficient than the OLS estimators $\hat{b_j}$ obtained from the untransformed equation
  • we must remember to in- terpret the estimates in light of the original equation

    • The WLS estimators are the values of the coefficients $b_j$ that minimize the following sum:

    $$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( y_i - b_0 - b_1 x_{i1} - b_2 x_{i2} - \dots - b_k x_{ik} \right)^2 / h_i $$

    or $$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( y_i - b_0^* - b_1 x_{i1}^* - b_2 x_{i2}^* - \dots - b_k x_{ik}^* \right)^2 $$

    Interpretation
    • The greater uncertainty, the smaller weight

    Estimating variation in the variance

    Feasible GLS (FGLS) estimator

    The variance of the error term $ \text{Var}(\mathbf{u}_0 | \mathbf{x}_2) $ is modeled by:

    $$ \text{Var}(\mathbf{u}_0 | \mathbf{x}_2) = \sigma^2 \exp\left( d_0 + d_1 x_1 + d_2 x_2 + \cdots + d_k x_k \right) $$

    Where:

    • $ \sigma^2 $ is the variance,
    • $ d_0, d_1, d_2, \dots, d_k $ are parameters,
    • $ x_1, x_2, \dots, x_k $ are the explanatory variables.
    Procedure to Correct for Heteroskedasticity (FGLS)

    1. Run the Initial Regression:

      • Estimate the original model using ordinary least squares (OLS): $$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k + u $$ Obtain the residuals from this regression, denoted as $ \hat{u} $.

    2. Create the Log of Squared Residuals:

      • Square the OLS residuals ($ \hat{u}^2 $) and then take the natural logarithm of the squared residuals: $$ \log(\hat{u}^2) $$

    3. Run the Regression of the Squared Residuals:

      • Regress the transformed squared residuals $ \log(\hat{u}^2) $ on the explanatory variables $ x_1, x_2, \dots, x_k $, which results in the fitted values $ \hat{g} $: $$ \log(\hat{u}^2) = d_0 + d_1 x_1 + d_2 x_2 + \cdots + d_k x_k $$

    4. Exponentiate the Fitted Values:

      • Exponentiate the fitted values $ \hat{g} $ from the previous step to obtain $ \hat{h}_i $: $$ \hat{h}_i = \exp(\hat{g}_i) $$

    5. Weighted Least Squares (WLS) Estimation:

      • Estimate the original regression equation again, but this time using Weighted Least Squares (WLS), where each observation is weighted by the inverse of $ \hat{h}_i $. The new regression equation becomes: $$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k + u $$ with the weights being $ \frac{1}{\hat{h}_i} $. This corrects for the heteroskedasticity in the error term.

      • Alternatively, you can first transform all variables (including the dependent variable and the explanatory variables) by dividing each by $ \sqrt{\hat{h}_i} $ and then run an OLS regression. In this case, each variable gets multiplied by $ \frac{1}{\sqrt{\hat{h}_i}} $, including the intercept.

    Summary

    1. We found the estimator of the regression coefficients, robust to heteroskedasticity, in the simple and multiple linear regression. With this new estimator, we repeated the hypothesis testing $\beta_i \ne 0$ in a conventional way. The presence of heteroskedasticity was illustrated by the visualization of residuals against predicted dependent variable for the considered example. BreuschPagan test is used to reveal heteroskedasticity quantitatively.
    2. We introduced Weighted Least Squares estimation. Initially we consider the problem assuming a specific form in which the variance varies. Then we estimate this from based on data.
