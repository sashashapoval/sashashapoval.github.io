Multiple Linear Regression

In this notebook, we study the multiple linear regression (MLR) model, which generalizes the simple regression case by allowing several explanatory variables.

We assume the following model:

\begin{equation} y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + \beta_2 x_{i2} + \cdots + \beta_k x_{ik} + u_i, \quad i = 1, \ldots, n. \label{e:mult:regr:mod} \end{equation}

Here:

  • $y_i$ dependent variable (response)
  • $x_{ij}$ explanatory variables (regressors)
  • $\beta_j$ unknown parameters to be estimated
  • $u_i$ random error term

We begin by presenting the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) estimator, including the equations for estimating the regression coefficients, and discuss the conditions under which these estimators are unbiased and efficient.

The tutorial then covers inference methods, such as the construction of confidence intervals for coefficients and the use of t-tests and F-tests for hypothesis testing, allowing for rigorous evaluation of the significance of individual regressors or groups of regressors.

Emphasis is placed on both the theoretical foundations and practical implementation, equipping readers with the tools to estimate, interpret, and test multiple regression models accurately in real-world applications.

Estimates for parameters

The Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) estimator minimizes the sum of squared residuals

\begin{equation} \min\limits_{\beta} \ \ S(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_{i1} - \cdots - \beta_k x_{ik})^2. \label{e:mult:regr:minimizaton} \end{equation}

The solution (in matrix form) is

\begin{equation} \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{y}, \label{e:mult:regr:solution} \end{equation}

where $\mathbf{X}$ is the $n \times (k+1)$ matrix of regressors (including a column of ones).

The $i$-th element of $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ is denoted by $\hat{\beta}_i$, and it can be written as a linear combination of the observed $y$ values:

$$ \hat{\beta}_i = \sum_{j=1}^n w_{ij} y_j, $$

where the weights come from the $i$-th row of the matrix $(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}'$:

$$ w_{ij} = \big[(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}'\big]_{ij}. $$

Thus, each coefficient estimate $\hat{\beta}_i$ is a linear function of the dependent variable $\mathbf{y}$.

Assumptions

  • Zero conditional mean assumption (setting correspondence between \eqref{e:mult:regr:mod} and \eqref{e:mult:regr:minimizaton}:
    • \begin{equation} E(\mathbf{u} \mid \mathbf{X}) = \mathbf{0}. \label{e:assumption:zero:cond_mean} \end{equation}
  • The absence of perfect collinearity (allowing for the existence of the solution of \eqref{e:mult:regr:minimizaton}, mind the denominator in \eqref{e:mult:regr:solution}:
    • \begin{equation} \text{rank}(\mathbf{X}) = k + 1 \quad\text{or equivalently}\quad \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X} \ \text{ is invertible}. \label{e:assumption:non-collinearity} \end{equation}

Decomposition of the total variation and the $R^2$ statistic

This part coincides with that know for a simple regression

In multiple linear regression, we can decompose the total variation in the dependent variable $y_i$ into two parts: the variation explained by the regression and the unexplained variation (residuals).

We define three fundamental sums of squares:

  • Total Sum of Squares (SST): $$ SST = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \bar{y})^2 $$

  • Explained Sum of Squares (SSE): $$ SSE = \sum_{i=1}^n (\hat{y}_i - \bar{y})^2 $$

  • Residual Sum of Squares (SSR): $$ SSR = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 $$

These quantities satisfy the fundamental identity: $$ SST = SSE + SSR. $$

Coefficient of determination ($R^2$)

The proportion of the total variation in $y$ explained by the regression is measured by the coefficient of determination:

$$ R^2 = \frac{SSE}{SST} = 1 - \frac{SSR}{SST}. $$

Interpretation:

  • $R^2 = 1$: perfect fit all observations lie exactly on the regression surface.
  • $R^2 = 0$: the model explains none of the variation in $y$.
  • In general, higher $R^2$ indicates better model fit, but adding more regressors will always (weakly) increase $R^2$.
Adjusted coefficient of determination ($R^2$)
Warning: $R^2$ always increases (or stays the same) when you add more variables even if those variables are useless.

To correct this, adjusted R penalizes the addition of irrelevant predictors.

Formula:

$$ R^2_{adj} = 1 - (1 - R^2)\frac{n - 1}{n - p - 1} $$

where

  • $n$ = number of observations,
  • $p$ = number of predictors (not counting the intercept).

Example (intuition):
If you fit a regression model and get:

  • $R^2 = 0.90$
  • $R^2_{adj} = 0.82$

that means your model explains 90% of the variance, but after accounting for model complexity, the true explanatory power is closer to 82%.

Relation Between the Simple and Multiple Regressions

Regression with an omitted term
If one relevant variable (say $x_2$) is omitted from the model, the simple regression coefficient $\tilde{\beta}_1$ can be biased. Suppose that $x_2$ is regressed on $x_1$ with the coefficient (slope) $\delta_1$: $x_2 = \delta_0 + \delta_1 x_1 + \text{err}$

Then, when we estimate the simple regression

$$ y_i = \tilde{\beta}_0 + \tilde{\beta}_1 x_{i1} + \text{resid}_i, $$

the estimated coefficient $\tilde{\beta}_1$ reflects both the direct effect of $x_1$ on $y$ (through $\beta_1$) and the indirect effect through its correlation with $x_2$ (via $\beta_2 \delta_1$).

Omitted Variable Bias (OVB)

The expected relationship between the simple and multiple regression coefficients is:

$$ E[\tilde{\beta}_1] = \beta_1 + \beta_2 \delta_1. $$

Thus, if $x_1$ and $x_2$ are correlated ($\delta_1 \neq 0$) and $x_2$ affects $y$ ($\beta_2 \neq 0$), then $\tilde{\beta}_1$ will be biased for $\beta_1$.

Statistical Inference

Statistical inference

From the OLS results, we can obtain:

  • Estimated coefficients $\hat{\beta}_j$
  • Standard errors $se(\hat{\beta}_j)$
  • $t$-statistics: $$ t_j = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{se(\hat{\beta}_j)}, $$ where $\beta_{j,0}$ is the hypothesized value (usually $0$)
  • Confidence intervals: $$ \hat{\beta}_j \pm t_{1-\alpha/2, \, n-k-1} \cdot se(\hat{\beta}_j) $$

We can also compute the $R^2$ statistic, F-statistic, and p-values to assess model fit.

Unbiasedness of the OLS estimator

Key Assumption
The crucial assumption for unbiasedness is the zero conditional mean assumption: $$ E[\mathbf{u} \mid \mathbf{X}] = \mathbf{0}. $$

That is, the error term has zero expected value for every combination of regressors equivalently, the regressors are exogenous.

Proof
Estimator $$ \begin{equation} \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'(\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \mathbf{u}) = \boldsymbol{\beta} + (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{u}. \label{e:mult:est} \end{equation} $$

Take the expectation conditional on $\mathbf{X}$ ($u$ is a random variable):

$$ E[\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \mid \mathbf{X}] = E[\boldsymbol{\beta} + (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{u} \mid \mathbf{X}] = \boldsymbol{\beta} + (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}' \cdot \underbrace{E[\mathbf{u} \mid \mathbf{X}]}_{\text{(assumption) } = 0} = \boldsymbol{\beta}. $$

Taking unconditional expectation (since this holds for any $\mathbf{X}$):

$$ E[\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}] = \boldsymbol{\beta}. $$

Hence, each OLS coefficient $\hat{\beta}_i$ is an unbiased estimator of the true parameter $\beta_i$.

Intuition
  • The estimator $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ is the true $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ plus a term that depends on the random errors $\mathbf{u}$.
  • If the regressors are uncorrelated with the errors (i.e., $E[\mathbf{u}|\mathbf{X}]=0$), the noise term has zero mean.
  • Therefore, on average, OLS recovers the true parameters.
$$\newcommand{\Var}{\mathrm{Var}}$$

Variance is equal to

$$ \Var(\mathbf{u} \mid \mathbf{X}) = \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}_n. $$
Key Assumption: Homoskedasticity and absence of serial correlation:
$$ \begin{equation} \Var(\hat{\beta}_i \mid \mathbf{X}) = \sigma^2 {\big[ (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} \big]}_{ii}. \label{e:mult:var} \end{equation} $$
Proof

Computing the variance of \eqref{e:mult:est}, where only the second term contributes to the variance because $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is a constant: $$ \Var(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \mid \mathbf{X}) = \Var\big((\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{u} \,\big|\, \mathbf{X}\big). $$

Applying the key assumption:

$$ \Var(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \mid \mathbf{X}) = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}' (\sigma^2 \mathbf{I}_n) \mathbf{X} (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}. $$$$ = \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}. $$

Therefore, each coefficient $\hat{\beta}_i$ has variance given by the diagonal elements of this matrix. This proves \eqref{e:mult:var}

Intuition
  • The more variation and less multicollinearity in $\mathbf{X}$, the smaller $(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$, and hence the smaller the sampling variance.
  • The variance is proportional to the error variance $\sigma^2$ noisier data less precise estimates.
From variance to standard error

In scalar form for multiple regression, we can write:

$$ \operatorname{Var}(\hat{\beta}_1) = \frac{\sigma^2}{SST_{1}(1 - R_1^2)}, $$

so the standard error is:

$$ \operatorname{se}(\hat{\beta}_1) = \sqrt{ \frac{\hat{\sigma}^2}{SST_{1}(1 - R_1^2)} }. $$

Where:

  • $SST_1 = \sum (x_{1i} - \bar{x}_1)^2$: total variation in $x_1$
  • $R_1^2$ is the $R^2$ from regressing $x_1$ on the other regressors
  • $\hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{SSR}{n-k-1}$: estimated variance of the error term

Normality assumption

The error term is normally distributed conditional on the regressors:

$$ u_i \mid X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2). $$

This implies that, given the explanatory variables, the disturbances follow a normal distribution with mean zero and constant variance.

    GaussMarkov Theorem

    The GaussMarkov assumptions are:
    1. Linearity: the model is linear in parameters.
    2. No autocorrelation: errors are uncorrelated.
    3. Full rank: $X$ has full column rank $k$.
    4. Zero mean errors: $\mathbb{E}[u \, | \, X] = 0$.
    5. Homoskedasticity: $\mathrm{Var}(u) = \sigma^2 I_n$.
  • Question:
    • Where did we use the full rank assumption?
    • Can we say that the expected value of $u$ is independent on $X$?
    • How do you understand 2?
    • To find the coefficients $\beta$. The formula contains $(X'X)^{-1}$. The assumption yields that the matrix $X'X$ is invertible.
    • Yes, based on 3.
    • Random variables $u_i$, $i=1,\ldots,n$, are uncorrelated: $\mathrm{Cov}(u_i, u_j) = 0$.
    Theorem (GaussMarkov):

    Under assumptions 15, the ordinary least squares (OLS) estimator

    $$ \hat{\beta} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y $$

    is the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator (BLUE) of $\beta$.

    This means:

    • Linear: $\hat{\beta}$ is a linear function of $y$.
    • Unbiased: $\mathbb{E}[\hat{\beta}] = \beta$.
    • Best: Among all linear unbiased estimators $\tilde{\beta}$,
    $$ \mathrm{Var}(\tilde{\beta}) - \mathrm{Var}(\hat{\beta}) \text{ is positive semidefinite.} $$

    With, additionally, the normality assumption,

    OLS estimators have exact finite-sample distributions:

    1. Estimator distribution: $$ \hat{\beta}_j \mid X \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left( \beta_j,\; \sigma^2 [(X'X)^{-1}]_{jj} \right). $$

    2. Estimator of error variance: $$ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n-k-1} \sum_{i=1}^n \hat{u}_i^2. $$

    3. t-statistics follow a Student t distribution: $$ t_j = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j} {\sqrt{\hat{\sigma}^2[(X'X)^{-1}]_{jj}}} \sim t_{n-k-1}. $$

    4. F-statistics used for joint hypothesis tests follow an F distribution with $(q,\, n-k-1)$ degrees of freedom. (to be discussed)

    def format_regression_output(results):
    """
    Print a statsmodels OLS regression in Stata-style:
    - Model equation with coefficients
    - Coefficients with standard errors underneath
    - t-statistics and p-values
    - Number of observations, SSR, and R-squared
    """

    params = results.params
    ses = results.bse
    t_stats = results.tvalues
    p_values = results.pvalues
    dep_var = results.model.endog_names

    # ----- Build regression equation -----
    eq = f"{dep_var} = "

    # Intercept first (if present)
    if "Intercept" in params.index:
        eq += f"{params['Intercept']:.4f}"
    else:
        eq += "0"

    # Add other variables
    for var in params.index:
        if var == "Intercept":
            continue
        sign = "+" if params[var] >= 0 else "-"
        eq += f" {sign} {abs(params[var]):.4f}*{var}"

    # ----- Print equation -----
    print("Model equation:\n")
    print("  " + eq + "\n")

    # ----- Print coefficients + SEs + t-statistics + p-values -----
    print("Coefficients (standard errors in parentheses, t-statistics and p-values in brackets):\n")
    for var in params.index:
        print(f"{var:12} {params[var]: .4f}")
        print(f"{'':12} ({ses[var]:.4f}) [t={t_stats[var]:.3f}, p={p_values[var]:.3f}]")
    print()

    # ----- Print model statistics -----
    n = int(results.nobs)
    ssr = float((results.resid ** 2).sum())
    r2 = float(results.rsquared)

    print(f"Number of observations (n): {n}")
    print(f"Sum of squared residuals (SSR): {ssr:.4f}")
    print(f"R-squared: {r2:.4f}\n")

    To get the standard error of the linear combination

    $$ H_0: \beta_{\text{jc}} - \beta_{\text{univ}} = 0 $$

    you need the variance of the difference:

    $$ \operatorname{Var}(\beta_{\text{jc}} - \beta_{\text{univ}}) = \operatorname{Var}(\beta_{\text{jc}}) + \operatorname{Var}(\beta_{\text{univ}}) - 2\,\operatorname{Cov} (\beta_{\text{jc}}, \beta_{\text{univ}}) $$

    Then the standard error is:

    $$ se(\beta_{\text{jc}} - \beta_{\text{univ}}) = \sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(\beta_{\text{jc}} - \beta_{\text{univ}})} $$ 
    # covariance matrix of coefficients
cov = results.cov_params()

# extract variances and covariance
var_jc   = cov.loc["jc",   "jc"] 
var_univ = cov.loc["univ", "univ"]
cov_jc_univ = cov.loc["jc", "univ"]

# compute standard error of the difference
se_diff = np.sqrt(var_jc + var_univ - 2 * cov_jc_univ)

print("Standard error of (beta_jc - beta_univ):", se_diff)

t_stat = beta_diff / se_diff

    Using an F-Statistic to Test Joint Significance

    Consider the following model explaining Major League Baseball players' salaries (Wooldridge):

    $$ \begin{multline*} \log(\text{salary}) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \,\text{years} + \beta_2 \,\text{gamesyr} + \beta_3 \,\text{bavg} \\ + \beta_4 \,\text{hrunsyr} + \beta_5 \,\text{rbisyr} + u, \end{multline*} $$

    where:

    • salary: 1993 total salary
    • years: years in the league
    • gamesyr: average games played per year
    • bavg: career batting average (e.g., 250 means .250)
    • hrunsyr: home runs per year
    • rbisyr: runs batted in per year

    Suppose we want to test the null hypothesis that, once years and gamesyr have been controlled for, the performance statistics bavg, hrunsyr, and rbisyr have no effect on salary.

    In words, the null hypothesis states that productivity, as measured by these baseball statistics, has no effect on salary.

    In terms of the model parameters, the null hypothesis is:

    $$ H_0:\; \beta_3 = 0, \quad \beta_4 = 0, \quad \beta_5 = 0. $$

    F-Statistic answers:

    Consider the general multiple regression model:

    $$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k + u \tag{4.34} $$

    Suppose we want to test the null hypothesis that a subset of $q$ coefficients are zero:

    $$ H_0: \beta_{k-q+1} = 0, \;\; \dots, \;\; \beta_k = 0 \tag{4.35} $$

    This imposes $q$ exclusion restrictions on the model. The alternative hypothesis is simply that $H_0$ is false, i.e., at least one of the $q$ coefficients is different from zero.

    When we impose these restrictions under $H_0$, we obtain the restricted model:

    $$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \cdots + \beta_{k-q} x_{k-q} + u $$

    Let:

    • $SSR_R$ = sum of squared residuals from the restricted model
    • $SSR_U$ = sum of squared residuals from the unrestricted model
    • $n$ = number of observations
    • $q$ = number of restrictions

    Then the F-statistic for testing $H_0$ is:

    $$ F = \frac{(SSR_R - SSR_U)/q}{SSR_U / (n - k - 1)} $$

    where $k$ is the total number of regressors in the unrestricted model (excluding the intercept).

    If $H_0$ is true, $F$ follows an $F$ distribution with $(q, n-k-1)$ degrees of freedom:

    $$ F \sim F_{q, n-k-1} $$ 
    # SSR from unrestricted model (results already fitted)
SSR_U = float(results.ssr)

# Fit the restricted model (excluding bavg, hrunsyr, rbisyr)
reg_restricted = smf.ols('lsalary ~ years + gamesyr', data=twoyear)
res_restricted = reg_restricted.fit()

# Number of restrictions
q = 3

# Total regressors in unrestricted model (excluding intercept)
k = 5

# Number of observations
n = int(results.nobs)

# Compute F-statistic
F_stat = ((SSR_R - SSR_U)/q) / (SSR_U / (n - k - 1))
print(f"Manual F-statistic: {F_stat:.4f}")

    The outcome of the joint test may seem surprising given the individual insignificant t-statistics for the three variables. Here is what is happening:

    1. Multicollinearity:
      The two variables hrunsyr and rbisyr are highly correlated. This multicollinearity makes it difficult to uncover the partial effect of each variable individually. This is why the individual t-statistics are small.

    2. Joint significance testing:
      The F-statistic tests whether the three variables (bavg, hrunsyr, and rbisyr) are jointly significant.

    3. Effect of multicollinearity on the F-test:
      Multicollinearity between hrunsyr and rbisyr is much less relevant when testing the joint hypothesis. Therefore, even if the individual t-statistics are insignificant, the F-test can still reject the null hypothesis if the variables together have explanatory power.

    $$ H_0:\, \beta_1 = \beta_2 = \ldots \beta_n = 0 $$

    Detecting multicollinearity: Variance Inflation Factors (VIFs)

    Multicollinearity occurs when some explanatory variables are highly linearly correlated.
    This inflates the variances of the estimated coefficients and makes statistical inference unreliable.

    To detect multicollinearity, we compute the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) for each regressor:

    $$ \mathrm{VIF}_j = \frac{1}{1 - R_j^2}, $$

    where $R_j^2$ is the coefficient of determination from regressing variable $x_j$ on all the other explanatory variables.

    Interpretation:

    • $\mathrm{VIF}_j = 1$: No multicollinearity.
    • $\mathrm{VIF}_j > 5$: Moderate multicollinearity.
    • $\mathrm{VIF}_j > 10$: Serious multicollinearity concern.

    In practice, we compute VIFs for all predictors (excluding the intercept).

    from statsmodels.stats.outliers_influence import variance_inflation_factor

# Compute VIFs for each explanatory variable
X_vif = X.drop(columns=['const'])  # exclude intercept if added
vif_data = pd.DataFrame()
vif_data['Variable'] = X_vif.columns
vif_data['VIF'] = [variance_inflation_factor(X_vif.values, i)
                   for i in range(X_vif.shape[1])]
